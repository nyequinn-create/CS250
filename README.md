# CS250

Reflection on Software Development Practices

Interpreting User Needs & User Stories
Interpreting user needs starts with understanding the problem from the user’s perspective rather than jumping straight into code. I focus on identifying what the user is trying to accomplish, what constraints they have, and what a successful outcome looks like. Creating user stories helps translate those needs into actionable development tasks. By framing requirements as “As a user, I want… so that…,” user stories keep development centered on real value and make it easier to prioritize features. They also improve communication within the team by breaking down complex requirements into smaller, testable goals.

Approach to Developing Programs & Agile Practices
When developing programs, I start with planning and design before implementation. I break problems into smaller components, evaluate possible solutions, and then build incrementally. Agile practices support this approach by encouraging iterative development, continuous feedback, and adaptability. In the future, I hope to incorporate practices such as sprint planning, daily stand-ups, retrospectives, and maintaining a prioritized product backlog. These processes help ensure steady progress, quick adjustments to changing requirements, and consistent delivery of working software.

Being a Good Team Member in Software Development
Being a good team member means contributing not just through code, but through communication, collaboration, and accountability. This includes clearly sharing ideas, being open to feedback, and supporting teammates when challenges arise. It also means taking responsibility for assigned tasks and ensuring they are completed with quality. A strong team member respects different perspectives, helps maintain a positive working environment, and stays focused on the shared goal of delivering a successful product.
